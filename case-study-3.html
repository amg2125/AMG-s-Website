Death of Innocence

  • Media Type: Video
  • Individual Project
  • Tools Used: Adobe After Effects, Illustrator, Premier

This animated short was created for a class called "Intermediate Animation: Motion Graphics and VFX." For the final project of the course, students had to individually create a 2-3 minute animated short films using Adobe After Effects. Students could choose whether they wanted their short films to be narrative films or explainer videos, and they were responsible for creating the film from start to finish and implementing the 12 Principles of Animation that we learned in class.

Development

Finding the Inspiration

The first step in development was choosing the subject for my animated short. Since I had already created an explainer video for the previous animation course, I decided to try my hand at creating a narrative short film. While exploring potential stories to base my film on, I thought about a project I had created for my "2D Character Design" course called "Death of Innocence." It was a pitch for an adaptation of the book "All Quiet on the Western Front" by Erich Maria Remarque, but set in the modern day during the Russo-Ukariainian War. The main character for my version was called Boris Sperkaz, a Ukrainian college student who was drafted into the Ukrainian army upon the outbreak of the war. I thought this would be a good story to adapt into an animated short because it was relevant and I already had a lot of materials at hand for it, such as character designs and a basic outline of the story.

Script and Storyboard

After I had settled on the subject of my animated short, I set out to create a script and storyboard for it. Since I had a limited amount of time to work with for my short, I decided to structure the video as if it was a trailer for a longer film. I wrote a script with Boris narrating how his life changed when the war came, and my storyboard consisted of a series of scenes showing Boris's life before and during the war. When planning out the storyboard, I thought carefully about what shots I wanted to include, how they would flow into each other, and how to connect the dialogue with the visuals. I also made sure to revise the storyboard and script as I progressed through production, taking into account time constraints and the experience I had with After Effects.

The first page of the storyboard for "Death of Innocence"

Creating the Assets

After I had the storyboard and script planned out, I started creating all the assets for the video. I used Adobe Illustrator to draw all the characters, objects, and backgrounds for the short film, using different visual references for each. For instance, when I was building Boris's model in Illustrator, I used the pencil sketches I had drawn of him in my 2D Character Design class as reference. Building the assets took some time because there were so many of them I had to create for the whole film. Some assets didn't take much time to create while others required a lot more time, especially if they belonged to a character that appeared in multiple scenes or needed to be expressive. I made multiple versions of Boris's character model, and I had to make sure every part of the model was shaped and parented correctly so that the model could be animated properly. I knew that I couldn't wait to animate until I had all the assets created, so I began animating the scenes I already had the assets for and kept working on the rest of the assets as I went along.

Narration and Sound Effects

While I was building the assets in Illustrator, I was also looking for sound effects to add into the film. I needed a lot of sound effects for the all the different scenes, and luckily I was able to find and download a lot of sound effects on YouTube and other stock sound effect websites. In addition to the sound effects, I also needed to record Boris's narration for the film. I didn't have time to find someone to do the narration and I felt that reading it myself wouldn't have the effect I wanted, so I decided to use an AI text-to-speech program to create the narration. I used a website called ElevenLabs to generate the narration, and I simply had to copy and paste the script into the website and select the voice I wanted. After selecting the voice, I generated the audio clips in portions and downloaded the clips to my computer. The task of gathering the sound effects and narration didn't take long, which allowed me to focus more on finishing the assets and animating the scenes.

Animating with After Effects

The animation stage of production was the most important and time-consuming part of the project. I had experience animating with After Effects before, having done so for a previous class in 2D animation, but it was still a challenge to get everything animated. I had to make sure that all the assets were parented and layered correctly, utilize certain animation tools and concepts learned in class, and make sure that everything worked properly. The process of animating with After Effects involved moving or rotating the assets and adding keyframes to mark each movement. One of the most challenging scenes to animate was a scene where multiple Ukrainian soldiers are running across a rugged field during an attack. Instead of animating each soldier manually, I used an plugin for After Effects called Duik Angela that allowed me to create a puppet rig for each soldier and animate them without using keyframes. I never used Duik before, so I ran into a few issues with it early on, mainly with getting the rig to work properly. With some trial and error and help from the professor, I was able to get the rigs working and animate all the soldiers in the scene.

Editing and Finalization

After I completed animating all the scenes, the final step was to edit the whole film together in Premier Pro. I imported all the individual scenes into Premier and cut the whole film together. This was the easiest part of the project because I didn't have to do much editing outside of putting the scenes together. With this step completed, I exported the file and presented the completed film to the professor and the class on the last day of the course.

Final Deliverable

Takeaways

This project was a great opportunity to learn more about animating with After Effects through hands-on experience. In addition to using features and tools I was familiar with, I also got to use new features and plugins that I wasn't familiar with such as Duik Angela. One of the takeaways I took from this experience was that there are many more features, tools, and plugins that I can use to animate with After Effects, and that sometimes all it takes is a little bit of playing around to figure out how to use them. Another major takeaway I took from this project was that After Effects plugins can be just as difficult to use as as After Effects itself. Duik was frustrating to use at times, but I gained a lot of experience from it and learned it can be a powerful tool to save time and effort for animating.
