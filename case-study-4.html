School of Comm VR Tour

  • Media Type: Virtual Reality
  • Group Project
  • Tools Used: Unity, Illustrator, Audacity

This virtual reality experience was created for a course called "Immersive Storytelling". For the project, students had to create a VR experience that both told a story and was interactive. The students that I worked with on this project were Daniele Montefiori, Jack Ketchum, and Lejie Lin.

Development

Choosing the Concept

My group and I decided to theme our VR experience around a virtual introductory tour of the School of Communication at the University of Miami. We envisioned the experience as taking users through the various classrooms and buildings in the school and educating them on their uses through an invisible narrator. We also put a lot of thought into the purpose of the experience, wanting it to be geared towards incoming and prospective students who want to learn about the School of Communication and familiarize themselves with the campus. This familiarity would be built through interactive input from the users as they explored the virtual campus, as well as through a short quiz at the end of the tour to test their knowledge of what they learned.

Planning and Role Assigning

Everyone in the group was assigned a specific role. I was in charge of writing and recording the script and assisting with the creation of some of the UI assets and icons; Leiji was in charge of filming the School of Communication campus and integrating the footage and sound effects into Unity; Daniele was in charge of building the project in Unity and adding essential features (i.e., camera movement, player movement, etc.); and Jack was in charge of creating the UI for the experience as well as assisting both Daniele and Leiji with their respective tasks. We would work on the project in class on assigned workdays and convened virtually every weekend to discuss our progress. We also created a few shared documents to keep track of progress, roles, and deadlines. We used Google Sheets to create a timeline and checklist for each group member, created another Google Sheet to map out the story flow and the goals for each scene, and lastly, we made a visual storyboard in Figma to plan out the experience visually and order the scenes in the proper way. We also made a Google Drive folder to store the footage and assets we created for the project.

Scriptwriting and Voiceover Recording

My first task was writing the script and narration for the experience. I wrote dialogue that described each of the buildings in simple yet informative language, as well as different lines that would play depending on how well the user scored on the quiz. I also kept the project's tone in mind while writing the script, making sure my narration came across as friendly and approachable to the user, since our target audience was incoming students. After writing the script and making some revisions, I recorded the lines into Audacity and made subsequent edits and retakes as needed. Once all the lines were recorded, I exported them to the shared Google Drive folder so they could be imported to Unity.

UI Design and Creation

I also helped in designing the UI assets for the project, which involved creating custom buttons for certain interactions in the experience. Some of these buttons were to begin the quiz at the end of the tour, and others were to signify certain objects that the user could interact with like a computer or a 3D printer. I created the buttons and icons in Adobe Illustrator, complete with variants for static and pressed states, and exported them as PNG files which were then resized to match the dimensions needed for Unity.

Filming and Photography

From the beginning of the project, we envisioned using a mix of 360 degree video and aerial drone footage for the experience. The 360 degree footage would make up the majority of the experience, while the drone footage would act as an establishing shot. Over a couple of weeks, Leiji and Jack captured the footage needed for the project, using a special 360 degree camera and a drone. They filmed both overall shots of the School of Communication campus and isolated shots inside or around specific buildings. Once they had the footage, they uploaded it to the shared Google Drive folder.

Unity and Coding

Coding the project in Unity took a lot of time, which was why we got started with it right away. Daniele encountered a number of problems while coding which forced us to make some changes to the project time and time again. For example, we originally planned to open the experience with a wide aerial shot of the city of Miami (in the form of a 3D model), before zooming in on the School of Communication campus. However, the 3D model was too large in size and caused significant lag in the VR headset. We also planned to use 3D models to represent the buildings on the School of Communication campus, but these caused lag as well and required us to spend a disproportionate amount of time on the intro sequence, thus detracting from the overall experience. This forced us to remove the 3D models entirely and fully base the experience around the 360 videos for better performance. Despite these setbacks, Daniele and the rest of the team were able to successfully code the project with the most critical features and implemented all the footage, assets, and audio we created.

Compiling and Final Delivery

Daniele and Lejie compiled all the assets and footage into the Unity project, and after doing some user testing and troubleshooting, we published our project. Although there were no 3D graphics in the experience, it was still very immersive thanks to the 360 degree video and audio narration. The experience was also highly interactive, as users could explore the campus at their own pace and in no specific order, and the experience conveyed a strong sense of story thanks to the narration and our decision to end the experience with the player walking into the advising office. The quiz at the end of the tour didn't have any scoring or interactive elements, which had to be cut out due to time constraints, but we planned to implemenet them in a future version of the project. Despite the setabacks we faced, we were able to deliver a polished and engaging VR experience that met the initial goals we set out to achieve.

Final Deliverable

Takeaways

This project taught us about the value of assigning team roles and setting a timeline early on. Doing this saved us a lot of time and energy, especially when we ran into unexpected problems during development. Assigning roles and making a timeline also helped us stay in touch with each other and remain focused on our individual tasks. This project also taught us that time constraints and unexpected obstacles can lead to changes in the original vision of a project, but fortunately, we were able to adapt and deliver with what we had.
