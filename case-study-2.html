Atzok the Deserter

  • Media Type: Story Pitch Presentation
  • Individual Project
  • Tools Used: Adobe Photoshop, MediBang Paint

This was my final project for a 2D Character Design class. The instructions: create an original story and design three characters: a protagonist, a secondary character, and an antagonist. Each design had to show an understanding of character structure, visual storytelling, and iteration through feedback. The final deliverable was to be a presentation explaining the story, characters, and the evolution of their designs.

Development

Finding the Concept

I knew going in that I wanted to work in science fiction, one of my favorite story genres, but I initially struggled to come up with a concept. Drawing inspiration from Star Wars and other sci-fi stories, I eventually developed a narrative about an alien soldier named Atzok who deserts his post and flees into hiding—only to be hunted years later by his old comrades. I aimed to create a grounded story set in a larger universe, similar to "A New Hope."

Atzok - Protagonist

Atzok belongs to a reptilian alien race called the Klorn. Designing him was a challenge, since I had never drawn reptilian characters before. I researched real and fictional reptiles for inspiration and sought guidance from my professor, which helped me shape Atzok's look. I created multiple sketches, expressions, and a full-body turnaround with his final design.

D4A5 - Secondary Character

Atzok's companion and bodyguard, D4A5, is a robot with a vintage sci-fi feel. Inspired by 70s-80s sci-fi magazines and Star Wars concept art, I explored different directions before settling on a final design. This character went through the most design iterations, but the process helped me refined my skills and visual instincts.

Trosk - Antagonist

Trosk, one of Atzok's old comrades, remained loyal to the Klorn Army. He is a foil to Atzok—more aggressive and hardened by war. My initial design looked too similar to Atzok, but after getting feedback from the professor, I reworked Trosk's design to make him look more menacing and expereinced.

Final Deliverable

I compiled all my sketches, turnarounds, and design notes into a short, detailed slideshow. It included inspiration, story context, design evolution, and character in multiple poses and situations.

❮ ❯

Takeaways

This project taught me that character design is way more than just drawing: it involves storytelling, revision, and clarity for other arists. I also learned that feedback is crucial in the design process. Input from my professor helped refine my skills and improved the revision process. Finally, drawing characters from different angles, poses, and expressions deepened my understanding of form and consistency, which is essential for any character designer.
